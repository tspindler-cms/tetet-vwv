Sampan

sampan_open

Displacement: 800 kg - 1.2 tons (typical)
Length: 4.5 – 6.5 meters (14.7 – 21 ft)
Beam: 1.2 – 1.6 meters (3.9 – 5.2 ft)
Draft: 0.2 – 0.4 meters (0.6 – 1.3 ft)
Speed: 15 knots (27 km/h) with motor / 3 knots manual
Range: Riverine/Coastal (limited by fuel/stamina)
Crew: 1 – 4

Equipment:
1 × "Duoi Tom" (Shrimp Tail) Long-tail outboard motor
2 × Standing oars (for traditional cross-arm rowing)
1 × Woven bamboo canopy (removable)
Painted Prow Eyes (mystical defense)
Fishing nets / Produce storage (variable loadout)

Source: Regional Maritime Studies (Vietnam)


The Vietnamese Sampan is a light, flat-bottomed wooden craft ubiquitous in the Mekong Delta and coastal waterways. Distinctive for the "Spirit Eyes" painted on the prow, intended to scare away crocodiles and water monsters. The design is often referred to as "Xuồng Ba Lá," literally translating to "three-plank boat," referencing its simple hull construction. While traditionally powered by standing oars or sails, many modern variants have been retrofitted with "long-tail" motors for navigating swift river currents. They serve as the primary mode of transport for floating markets, fishing, and rural transit.
